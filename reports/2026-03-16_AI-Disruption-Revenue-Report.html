The headline finding: Only 14 of these 37 "AI-disrupted" companies have actually seen revenue decline. 18 grew revenue — some by 30–70%. Yet every single one (bar Omnicom) has seen its stock fall, many by 50–90%+. The stock market is pricing in future disruption that, for most of these companies, hasn't yet shown up in the top line. The fear of what AI will do is, so far, much larger than what it has done.

The Perception Gap: Stock Decline vs. Revenue Decline

For every company, the red bar shows stock price change and the blue bar shows revenue change. The gap between them is the market's "AI fear premium" — the decline attributed to anticipated future disruption rather than actual revenue loss.

What's Actually Happening

1. Real revenue casualties: AI is already hurting these businesses

The clearest cases of AI-driven revenue destruction are LivePerson (−54%), Chegg (−53%), and ZipRecruiter (−49%). LivePerson is the most ironic: an AI chatbot company destroyed by better AI chatbots. Chegg's homework-help business was essentially replaced overnight by ChatGPT. ZipRecruiter faces both AI automation of recruiting and a macro hiring slowdown.

The staffing sector — Robert Half (−26%), TrueBlue (−25%), Kelly Services (−20%), ASGN (−16%) — is seeing real revenue erosion, though it's hard to separate AI from cyclical factors. AI automates the matching/screening that staffing firms charge for, while simultaneously reducing the number of roles companies need to fill.

Forrester Research (−26%) and Perion Network (−22%) are clear cases: AI is commoditizing premium market research (Forrester), and Microsoft's AI pivot in Bing directly killed Perion's search advertising revenue.

2. The "fear premium" companies: Stock crushed, revenue fine

This is the more interesting group. Sprout Social's stock is down 89% despite revenue growing 77%. Globant is down 74% with revenue up 38%. Five9 is down 74% with revenue up 44%. Concentrix is down 71% with revenue up 57% (partly from its Webhelp merger). Freshworks is down 41% with revenue up 67%.

For these companies, investors are pricing in a future where AI agents replace human customer service reps (Five9, Concentrix), AI tools replace freelancers (Fiverr), or AI commoditizes SaaS tools (Sprout Social, Freshworks). The revenue hasn't declined yet, but growth is decelerating — and the market is forward-looking.

Even Adobe, which grew revenue 35% to nearly $6B/quarter, has seen its stock fall 24%. The market worries that AI image generation will eventually compress the need for complex creative software.

3. How stock declines relate to revenue declines

Companies with actual revenue declines saw an average stock drop of −77%. Companies that grew revenue still saw an average stock drop of −47%. So roughly half the stock price decline is fear — the market pricing in what AI might do — and the other half reflects real business deterioration.

The correlation between stock decline and revenue change exists but is loose: LivePerson (−54% revenue, −98% stock) and Chegg (−53% revenue, −98% stock) are at the extreme, but Sprout Social (−90% stock, +77% revenue) shows the market can panic about a company whose revenue is still growing rapidly.

4. Will revenue continue dropping?

For the real casualties — almost certainly yes. Chegg, LivePerson, and Forrester face structural replacement of their core value proposition by AI. There is no plausible recovery path to prior revenue levels. ZipRecruiter's decline may stabilize if hiring recovers, but AI is permanently compressing the value of job board intermediaries.

For the staffing firms — likely, but slowly. AI doesn't instantly eliminate temp staffing. It reduces demand gradually as companies automate more tasks. A recession would accelerate this.

For the "fear premium" companies — the critical question. If AI agents start replacing human customer service reps at scale (threatening Five9, Concentrix, TTEC), revenue for these companies could fall sharply. If AI coding tools substantially reduce demand for outsourced IT work (threatening EPAM, Globant, Endava), the fear will prove justified. Early signs are emerging: Globant's revenue growth stalled to ~1% in 2025, EPAM guided for just 4.5–7.5% growth in 2026 (vs. 15% in 2025), and Meta told TaskUs it would use AI to automate content moderation.

5. Perception vs. reality: Key caveats

Some "revenue growth" is misleading. Concentrix's 57% increase is mostly from acquiring Webhelp, not organic growth. Shutterstock's 27% increase includes ~$100M in one-off AI data licensing deals (e.g., the $250M OpenAI contract). Strip those out and the underlying business is eroding.

Growth deceleration matters as much as decline. Sprout Social's revenue is still growing, but growth decelerated from 31% (2023) to 13% (2025) to a projected 8% (2026). Freshworks decelerated from 20%+ to 14%. The market often punishes decelerating growth as harshly as actual decline, because it signals the beginning of a structural shift.

Not all of this is AI. Staffing declines correlate with cyclical hiring slowdowns. TripAdvisor was struggling before ChatGPT. Some BPO companies face macro headwinds. But AI provides the narrative that makes investors treat cyclical weakness as structural, permanently re-rating these stocks lower.
